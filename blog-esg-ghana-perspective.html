ESG: The Ghana Perspective

Understanding how Environmental, Social, and Governance factors integrate into Ghana's legal framework and why implementing ESG is crucial for sustainable business success in the Ghanaian context.

Article Information

Title of Original Work: ESG: The Ghana Perspective
Author(s): Ewurama Osam Tawiah, Lady-Ann Essuman, Verissa Odame-Koranteng
Publication Source: Dennis Law News
Year: 2023
Full Reference: Tawiah, E. O., Essuman, L.-A., & Odame-Koranteng, V. (2023, September 10). ESG: The Ghana Perspective. Dennis Law News. https://dennislawnews.com/opinions-and-features-v2/esg-the-ghana-perspective
Governance Area: ESG • Sustainability • Corporate Governance • Ghana Legal Framework

The Core Thesis

While Ghana's laws may not explicitly mention ESG, the underlying principles are embedded within the country's legal framework through various acts and regulations. ESG goes beyond corporate governance by incorporating environmental and social factors alongside governance practices. For Ghanaian businesses, implementing ESG factors is not optional—it is essential for sustainability, especially given the cultural context where community engagement and stakeholder relationships are critical to business success.

How the Research Was Conducted

This article provides a comprehensive legal and practical analysis of ESG implementation in Ghana. The authors examine Ghana's existing legal framework, including the Constitution, Companies Act 2019 (Act 992), Environmental Protection Act 1994 (Act 490), Minerals and Mining Act 2006 (Act 703), Labor Act 2003 (Act 651), and sector-specific regulations like the Bank of Ghana's Sustainable Banking Principles. The analysis explores how ESG principles align with existing laws, distinguishes ESG from corporate governance and CSR, and provides practical examples from various industries including mining, banking, and SMEs to illustrate the importance of ESG implementation in the Ghanaian context.

Key Findings & Discussion

  • •

    ESG is Embedded in Ghana's Legal Framework

    Although ESG is not explicitly mentioned in Ghanaian laws, the principles are incorporated through various legislative instruments. The Constitution, Companies Act 2019, Environmental Protection Act, Minerals and Mining Act, Labor Act, and sector-specific regulations like the Bank of Ghana's Sustainable Banking Principles all contain elements that align with ESG objectives. Regulatory bodies such as the Environmental Protection Agency, Labour Commission, and Bank of Ghana are tasked with enforcing various aspects of ESG, demonstrating that the framework exists even if not labeled as such.

  • •

    ESG is More Comprehensive Than Corporate Governance

    While corporate governance focuses solely on good governance practices, ESG encompasses environmental, social, and governance factors. ESG includes environmental considerations like Environmental Impact Assessments, carbon emissions, deforestation, waste management, and water usage. It incorporates social indicators such as CSR practices, labor management, health and safety, human rights, and community engagement. The governance aspect of ESG ensures board diversity, senior management policies, cybersecurity, business ethics, tax transparency, stakeholder engagement, and risk management. Corporate governance is therefore a component of ESG, not equivalent to it.

  • •

    ESG Differs from CSR in Scope and Purpose

    Most Ghanaian businesses have incorporated Corporate Social Responsibility into their practices, but this does not compensate for the impact that ESG practices would have. CSR focuses on a business's accountability to itself, the community, and society through social and economic impact measures. ESG, however, considers social, environmental, and governance factors comprehensively. A company practicing ESG contributes to community development and sustainability through local employment opportunities, infrastructure investment, and training promotion, while ensuring methods that promote community inclusion and environmental protection, ultimately boosting organizational productivity.

  • •

    ESG is Essential for Business Sustainability in Ghana

    ESG serves as a framework that evaluates a company's sustainability performance. Sustainability involves managing environmental, social, and financial concerns to ensure responsible, ethical, and ongoing success. ESG aligns with the three pillars of sustainability: environmental (preserving natural resources, minimizing pollution, addressing climate change), social (promoting social well-being, human rights, inclusive communities), and economic (creating financial value while ensuring equitable resource distribution). ESG serves as a practical tool to promote sustainability by considering the implementation of environmental, social, and governance factors in business operations. A business needs ESG to be truly sustainable.

  • •

    Cultural Context Makes ESG Implementation Critical

    In Ghana, where culture heavily influences business practices, implementing ESG factors is crucial for business success. External implementation involves a business's conduct with its environment and stakeholders. For example, in mining, land may be owned by traditional areas requiring chief approval before government licensing, making community engagement essential. Internal implementation involves conduct with human resources. SMEs in Ghana are often run as one-person businesses, but to ensure sustainability, they must institute accountability measures, use policies to govern employees and financial reporting, and implement performance management and training systems. In banking, retaining customers requires interpersonal relations, local language engagement, and community activities to build trust and confidence. It is fatal to do business in Ghana without implementing ESG factors because a business will not be sustainable.

Governance Implications

This analysis provides critical guidance for boards and executives operating in Ghana:

  • Understand the legal foundation: While ESG may not be explicitly mentioned, boards should recognize that Ghana's legal framework already incorporates ESG principles. Companies should align their ESG practices with existing laws and regulations enforced by bodies like the EPA, Labour Commission, and Bank of Ghana.
  • Go beyond corporate governance: Boards should expand their focus beyond traditional governance practices to include environmental and social factors. This means considering Environmental Impact Assessments, carbon emissions, waste management, labor practices, community engagement, and stakeholder relationships as integral to governance.
  • Recognize ESG as distinct from CSR: Boards should understand that CSR activities alone are insufficient. ESG requires a comprehensive approach that integrates environmental, social, and governance factors into core business operations, not just philanthropic activities.
  • Address industry-specific ESG factors: Each industry has unique ESG considerations. Mining companies must engage with traditional authorities and communities. Food companies must focus on product safety. Banks must build trust through interpersonal relations and community engagement. Boards should identify and address their industry's specific ESG factors.
  • Implement both external and internal ESG: Boards should ensure ESG implementation extends beyond external stakeholder relations to include internal practices. For SMEs, this means moving from one-person operations to structured governance with accountability measures, policies, and performance management systems.
  • Integrate ESG with sustainability goals: Boards should use ESG as a framework to achieve sustainability objectives, ensuring that environmental, social, and governance factors work together to create long-term value and sustainable business practices.
  • Respect cultural context: In Ghana, cultural factors are critical. Boards must ensure that ESG implementation respects traditional authorities, engages communities appropriately, and builds trust through culturally appropriate methods, such as using local languages and participating in community activities.

Our Take: Why This Matters for SMEs in Africa

This article is particularly valuable for African businesses because it clarifies the relationship between ESG, corporate governance, and CSR—concepts that are often confused or used interchangeably. The distinction is crucial: corporate governance is a component of ESG, not equivalent to it, and CSR alone is insufficient for true sustainability. This understanding helps boards develop more comprehensive governance frameworks that address environmental and social factors alongside traditional governance practices.

The emphasis on cultural context is especially relevant for African businesses. The article's examples from mining (requiring traditional authority approval) and banking (using local languages and community engagement) demonstrate that ESG implementation in Africa must respect cultural norms and traditional structures. This is particularly important for SMEs, which often operate in close-knit communities where relationships and cultural respect are essential for business success. The finding that SMEs must move from one-person operations to structured governance with accountability measures provides a clear roadmap for growth and sustainability.

However, we note that while the article provides excellent legal and conceptual analysis, it could benefit from more quantitative evidence on the business impact of ESG implementation in Ghana. Future research should examine how ESG practices correlate with financial performance, customer retention, and community support in the Ghanaian context. Additionally, the article's focus on specific industries (mining, banking, food) suggests that sector-specific ESG frameworks may be needed, which could be explored further. The integration of ESG with existing legal frameworks is well-articulated, but practical implementation challenges and solutions could be expanded to help boards navigate the transition from traditional governance to comprehensive ESG frameworks.

The article's conclusion that "it is fatal to do business in Ghana without implementing ESG factors" is a strong statement that underscores the importance of ESG for business sustainability. This message is particularly relevant as African businesses seek to attract international investment, comply with global standards, and build sustainable operations that benefit both shareholders and communities. Boards across Africa should take note: ESG is not a luxury or optional add-on—it is essential for long-term business success and sustainability.
