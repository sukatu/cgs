Agency Costs and Corporate Governance in Ghana: What Works and What Doesn't

Research reveals which governance mechanisms actually reduce agency costs in Ghanaian companies—and which ones don't make a difference.

Article Information

Title of Original Work: Agency costs, Ownership Structure and Corporate Governance Mechanisms in Ghana
Author(s): Andrews Owusu & Charlie Weir
Publication Source: Coventry University & Robert Gordon University
Year: 2014
Full Reference: Owusu, A., & Weir, C. (2014). Agency costs, Ownership Structure and Corporate Governance Mechanisms in Ghana. SSRN Electronic Journal. https://ssrn.com/abstract=4180007
Governance Area: Agency Theory • Board Structure • Ownership Structure • Compliance

The Core Thesis

Not all corporate governance mechanisms are created equal. Research examining Ghanaian companies from 2000 to 2009 reveals that smaller boards, audit committees, and higher ownership stakes by managers and institutions effectively reduce agency costs. However, separating the CEO and board chair roles, and having more non-executive directors, showed no significant impact—challenging conventional governance wisdom.

How the Research Was Conducted

The researchers analyzed data from Ghanaian companies over a ten-year period (2000-2009), examining the relationship between various corporate governance mechanisms and agency costs. Agency costs were measured using standard financial indicators that capture the efficiency of how companies use their resources. The study looked at both board structure elements—like board size, committee presence, and director independence—and ownership patterns, including how much stock managers and institutions held. The analysis also considered the impact of the Ghanaian Corporate Governance Code introduced during this period, allowing researchers to see which governance practices actually worked in practice versus what was simply recommended.

Key Findings & Discussion

  • •

    Smaller Boards Work Better

    Companies with smaller boards experienced lower agency costs. This finding supports the idea that larger boards face coordination challenges and are easier for CEOs to control, reducing their effectiveness. Smaller boards enable faster decision-making, reduce free-riding among directors, and create more accountability. This is particularly relevant in Ghana's business environment where board meetings may be less frequent and coordination more challenging.

  • •

    Audit and Remuneration Committees Matter

    The presence of audit committees and remuneration committees significantly reduced agency costs. These specialized committees provide focused oversight on critical areas—financial reporting integrity and executive compensation. Audit committees help ensure accurate financial information reaches shareholders, while remuneration committees align executive pay with company performance. Their effectiveness suggests that targeted governance structures work better than general board oversight alone.

  • •

    Ownership Alignment Reduces Costs

    Higher levels of managerial ownership and institutional ownership both led to lower agency costs. When managers own more shares, their interests align more closely with shareholders. Similarly, institutional investors bring professional monitoring and have the resources to hold management accountable. This finding highlights that ownership structure can be as important as board structure in controlling agency problems.

  • •

    CEO Duality and Non-Executive Directors Show No Effect

    Surprisingly, separating the CEO and board chair positions, and having a higher proportion of non-executive directors, showed no significant impact on agency costs. This challenges the conventional wisdom that these mechanisms automatically improve governance. The researchers suggest that in Ghana's context, non-executive directors may lack the independence, information, or incentives to effectively monitor management. Similarly, CEO duality may not be problematic if other governance mechanisms are working effectively.

  • •

    The Ghanaian Code Made a Difference

    The introduction of the Ghanaian Corporate Governance Code played a significant role in reducing agency costs overall. Beyond board structure, the code's recommendations on improved shareholder voting rights, adoption of International Financial Reporting Standards, and higher auditor quality all contributed to better governance outcomes. This suggests that comprehensive governance reforms, not just board composition changes, are necessary for meaningful improvement.

Governance Implications

This research provides clear, actionable guidance for boards and CEOs in Ghana and similar African markets:

  • Prioritize board size over composition: Focus on keeping boards small and manageable rather than simply adding more non-executive directors. A smaller, engaged board is more effective than a large, disconnected one.
  • Establish specialized committees: Ensure audit and remuneration committees are not just paper exercises but active governance bodies with real authority and resources.
  • Consider ownership incentives: Boards should evaluate executive compensation structures that include meaningful equity ownership, aligning management interests with shareholders.
  • Engage institutional investors: Actively seek and work with institutional investors who can provide professional monitoring and governance oversight.
  • Don't over-rely on structural fixes: Simply separating CEO and chair roles or adding non-executive directors won't solve governance problems if other mechanisms aren't working.
  • Focus on comprehensive reform: Governance improvements require changes beyond board structure—including financial reporting standards, auditor quality, and shareholder rights.

Our Take: Why This Matters for SMEs in Africa

This research is particularly valuable for African companies because it challenges the assumption that copying governance structures from developed markets automatically improves performance. The finding that non-executive directors and CEO-chair separation had no effect suggests that in African contexts, these mechanisms may not function as intended due to cultural factors, limited director pools, or weak enforcement.

For small and medium enterprises (SMEs) in Ghana and across Africa, the practical implications are significant. Many SMEs struggle with governance because they try to implement complex board structures they can't properly resource or monitor. This research suggests SMEs should focus on what actually works: keeping boards small and focused, establishing clear committee structures, and ensuring management has skin in the game through ownership stakes.

However, we must note a limitation: this study focused on listed companies, which typically have more resources than SMEs. The challenge for smaller companies is implementing effective audit and remuneration committees with limited budgets. Future research should explore how these findings apply to smaller, unlisted companies that form the backbone of African economies. Additionally, the study period (2000-2009) predates recent governance reforms, suggesting that updated research might reveal different patterns as Ghana's governance infrastructure matures.
